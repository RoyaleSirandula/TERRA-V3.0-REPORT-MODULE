Wildfire is among the most immediate and indiscriminate threats to protected ecosystems. Driven by prolonged drought, illegal agricultural burning, and shifting climate patterns, uncontrolled fires can devastate thousands of hectares of critical habitat within hours — far outpacing the response capacity of ground-based ranger teams relying on visual observation and radio communication.

Terra's Fire Monitoring module integrates NASA FIRMS (Fire Information for Resource Management System) satellite detections directly into the operational dashboard — overlaying active fire perimeters, wind vector data, and historical burn patterns onto live reserve maps. Commanders can immediately see fire proximity to wildlife corridors, community boundaries, and watering holes — and dispatch multi-asset response teams in a coordinated, intelligence-led suppression operation.

01
Ground Detection Is Too Slow
The majority of protected areas in Sub-Saharan Africa still rely on visual ranger observation as the primary fire detection mechanism. In dense bush or remote terrain, a fire can burn for 12–36 hours before a ground team encounters it — by which time suppression is impossible and evacuation is the only option.
02
Uncoordinated Multi-Asset Response
When a fire is detected, the response commonly involves multiple agencies — national parks, neighbouring reserves, aviation contractors — each operating on separate radio channels with no shared operational picture. Asset conflicts, redundant coverage, and uncovered flanks are routine, directly worsening outcomes.
↗
03
Illegal Agricultural Burning
A significant proportion of large-scale bush fires in East and Southern Africa originate from uncontrolled agricultural burns along reserve boundaries — set deliberately or negligently by farming communities. Without spatial attribution tools, accountability and preventive engagement with those communities is near impossible to implement systematically.
04
Escalating Climate Conditions
Fire seasons across Sub-Saharan Africa are measurably lengthening. The window between the end of one fire season and the onset of the next — historically a period of vegetation recovery and fire risk reduction — is shrinking, leaving ecosystems with less resilience and reserve managers with no additional tools to address a fundamentally worsening threat environment.

1
Satellite Detection & Alerting
A new FIRMS fire detection within a monitored reserve boundary triggers an automatic Terra alert — pushed to the duty commander's dashboard within minutes of the satellite overpass. The alert includes coordinates, fire radiative power, and a preliminary wind-adjusted spread projection mapped directly onto the reserve.
2
Threat Assessment & Asset Tasking
The commander reviews the active fire layer against wildlife corridors, water sources, ranger post locations, and community boundaries. Using Terra's live map, they task specific assets — aerial units, ground vehicles, ranger bikes — and issue sector assignments with real-time tracking of each team's position.
↗
3
Coordinated Suppression Operation
All field assets operate from the same shared live map. Pilots relay updated fire perimeter observations directly into the system; ground teams mark completed firebreaks. The commander sees the full suppression operation unfold in real time — and can redirect assets dynamically as fire behaviour changes.
4
Post-Burn Audit & Risk Update
Once contained, the burned area is captured as a spatial record — updating the vegetation layer, logging ignition source attribution, and feeding burn history into the seasonal risk model. Each incident makes the next prediction more precise, building a compounding intelligence asset over time.
