Habitat loss is the leading driver of species extinction globally — outpacing even direct poaching in its long-term impact. Vast protected areas are frequently "paper parks" in practice: gazetted in law, but actively degraded by illegal logging, charcoal burning, and agricultural expansion that goes undetected for months because no spatial monitoring infrastructure exists at the reserve level.

Terra's Site Analysis module provides rangers, wardens, and regional analysts with a continuous, layered spatial view of their protected areas — integrating satellite-derived change detection, decentralised ground-truthing by field teams, and version-controlled collaborative mapping. Complex spatial queries that once required dedicated GIS analysts can now be answered in seconds from the field.

01
The GIS Bottleneck
Traditional GIS platforms (ArcGIS, QGIS) require specialist-level training. In most reserves, a single GIS analyst handles spatial queries for dozens of rangers — creating a bottleneck where urgent requests like "how many vehicles have crossed this boundary in 30 days?" take 24–72 hours to answer.
02
Cost Barriers to Entry
Enterprise-grade spatial platforms cost $10,000–$50,000 per licence annually — completely inaccessible to the community conservancies, small NGOs, and ranger units that protect the majority of Africa's biodiversity. Without spatial tools, they patrol blind.
↗
03
Encroachment Goes Undetected
In reserves without active change-detection infrastructure, illegal logging and agricultural clearing frequently goes unnoticed for 6–18 months. By the time a ground team identifies it, the deforestation front has already penetrated several kilometres into the protected zone.
04
Fragmented Corridor Data
Wildlife migration corridors that span multiple conservancies and national parks are rarely monitored holistically. Each entity holds their own data in incompatible formats, meaning the cross-boundary picture — essential for understanding species movement — is effectively invisible to any one manager.

1
Study Initiation
An analyst creates a new Site Study, defines the geographic boundary, and layers in the latest Sentinel-2 imagery. The study is automatically assigned a unique Membership ID, which all contributing rangers and collaborators use to reference and contribute to the shared spatial record.
2
Ground-Truth by Rangers
Rangers in the field open the study offline and mark suspected logging camps, illegal boreholes, and fence breaches directly onto the map from GPS. All contributions are tagged with the ranger's membership ID, timestamp, and device-captured evidence.
↗
3
Version-Controlled Approval
Each ranger's spatial contribution is pushed as a "branch" to the group study. The Chief Analyst reviews all proposed additions, accepts or rejects each contribution with documented reasoning, and merges approved data into the canonical reserve map — creating a full audit trail for governance and legal use.
4
Export to Policy & Research
Finalised studies are exportable to government enforcement agencies, IUCN reports, and university research platforms. Multi-year composite analyses form the backbone of biodiversity case studies, encroachment litigation evidence, and academic publications on biome health.
